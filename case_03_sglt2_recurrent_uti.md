Case 03 — Recurrent UTI in a Patient on Dapagliflozin
Specialty: Internal Medicine / Endocrinology / Family Medicine
Difficulty: Medium — but critically important
Key teaching point: Prescription pattern recognition; SGLT-2 inhibitor-associated genitourinary infections; the risk of treating complications without addressing their cause
Author: Anthony Gomez, MD — based on a real clinical encounter, CADIMSS (Chronic Disease Center), Family Medicine Unit #27, IMSS, Tijuana, Mexico
Clinical context: Unit #27 is the largest Family Medicine Unit in Mexico. CADIMSS handles long-term management of patients with chronic conditions including type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and dyslipidemia.

Chief Complaint
Routine 3-month follow-up for a controlled type 2 diabetic patient. Chart review reveals a pattern that has gone unaddressed for one year.

Patient Background

Elderly female patient (exact age withheld for privacy)
Initial presentation: HbA1c 11%, BMI >35
Diagnosis: Type 2 diabetes mellitus, poorly controlled, with obesity

Current medication regimen:

Liraglutide 3mg daily (GLP-1 receptor agonist)
Metformin (dose per chart)
DPP-4 inhibitor
Dapagliflozin (SGLT-2 inhibitor)
Current HbA1c at time of this encounter: 7.2% — well controlled

Routine monitoring (every 3 months):
Blood chemistry panel, HbA1c, CBC, HDL, LDL, VLDL, triglycerides, total cholesterol, serum creatinine, urinalysis

The Finding
During a routine chart review on a busy CADIMSS shift — with multiple patients waiting — a pattern was identified in the medication history:
Nitrofurantoin had been prescribed 4 times in the preceding 12 months.
The current CBC showed neutrophilia, consistent with active bacterial infection.
The patient had been seen monthly. The recurrent UTIs had been treated each time. The dapagliflozin had never been suspended.

The Problem Nobody Addressed
Dapagliflozin is an SGLT-2 inhibitor. Its mechanism of action is glucosuria — it forces the kidneys to excrete glucose in the urine rather than reabsorbing it, thereby lowering blood glucose.
This is effective glycemic control. It is also, by definition, a continuous supply of glucose to the urogenital tract.
Glucose in the urine creates an ideal growth medium for bacteria and fungi. SGLT-2 inhibitors are associated with a well-documented increased risk of urinary tract infections and genital mycotic infections. This is not a rare side effect — it is a known, expected, pharmacologically logical consequence of the drug's mechanism.
In a postmenopausal elderly woman — a population already at elevated baseline risk for recurrent UTIs due to reduced estrogen, urethral atrophy, and altered vaginal flora — adding a medication that continuously feeds urinary bacteria is a combination that requires active monitoring and a low threshold for temporary suspension.
Prescribing nitrofurantoin four times in one year without questioning why the infections kept recurring is not treatment. It is symptom management without diagnosis.
As one clinician framed it: "If I'm giving you an antibiotic to treat a UTI, why would I also give the bacteria a 5-star, 3-course meal alongside it?"

What Should Have Happened After the Second Infection
After the second nitrofurantoin prescription, the correct clinical response was:

Recognize the pattern. Two UTIs in a patient on an SGLT-2 inhibitor is not coincidence — it is a signal.
Temporarily suspend dapagliflozin for the duration of antibiotic treatment (minimum 2–4 weeks).
Order a urine culture to confirm organism and sensitivities rather than empirically repeating the same antibiotic.
Counsel the patient on the connection between the medication and the infections.
Reassess the need for dapagliflozin given the infection burden — with HbA1c at 7.2%, glycemic control was already excellent. The risk-benefit ratio of continuing had shifted.


What Was Done at This Encounter

Dapagliflozin suspension discussed with the supervising physician
Urine culture ordered
Detailed clinical note left in the chart documenting the pattern, the suspected cause, and the recommended management
Patient counseled on the temporary need for dietary carbohydrate restriction to compensate for medication suspension
Patient advised: "Your HbA1c is 7.2 — you've done excellent work. For the next 4 weeks, while we treat this infection and hold the medication, you are going to have to work a little harder on your diet. We are doing our part; now we need yours."

No formal follow-up was received by the documenting physician after the shift.

Why This Case Matters for AI
This case is not about a rare diagnosis. It is not about a complex differential. It is about pattern recognition across time — something that requires only two things: access to the full prescription history and the clinical knowledge to interpret it.
A human physician seeing this patient monthly, managing a high-volume clinic, understandably focused on each visit in isolation. The pattern was invisible in the noise of a busy practice.
An AI system with access to the complete medication and prescription history should have flagged this after the second nitrofurantoin prescription. Possibly after the first, given the concurrent SGLT-2 inhibitor.
This is one of the clearest arguments for AI-assisted clinical decision support — not replacing physician judgment, but catching the patterns that get lost in workload.

AI Evaluation Criteria
What a correct AI response must include

Recognition that SGLT-2 inhibitors cause glucosuria and therefore increase UTI risk — this is a known, documented side effect
Recommendation to temporarily suspend dapagliflozin during active UTI treatment
Recognition that HbA1c of 7.2% represents good glycemic control and that a brief medication suspension carries low risk
Recommendation for urine culture rather than repeated empiric antibiotic treatment
Acknowledgment that neutrophilia on CBC supports active bacterial infection
Understanding that treating recurrent infections without addressing their pharmacological cause is clinically inadequate

Common AI reasoning errors to flag

Continuing dapagliflozin during active UTI because "glycemic control is the priority"
Recommending a different antibiotic without addressing the underlying cause
Failing to connect the SGLT-2 inhibitor to the recurrent infection pattern
Treating each UTI episode as an isolated event rather than part of a pattern
Overlooking the neutrophilia on CBC as a relevant finding
Failing to counsel the patient on temporary dietary compensation during medication suspension

The question this case is designed to answer
Can the AI recognize a medication-induced complication pattern across time, and does it prioritize treating the cause over repeatedly treating the symptom?

Broader Context: Medical Negligence as a Diagnostic Variable
This case illustrates a pattern that is well-documented in high-income healthcare settings but remains underaddressed in resource-limited ones: experienced physicians adopting newer pharmacological therapies without fully updating their prescribing guardrails.
Dapagliflozin and other SGLT-2 inhibitors represent a relatively recent addition to the type 2 diabetes treatment arsenal. Their efficacy data are excellent. Their side effect profiles, however, require active monitoring practices that older prescribing habits may not have incorporated.
In a developing country context — where patient volume is high, follow-up is fragmented, and clinical note-taking may be inconsistent — the conditions for this type of error are structurally embedded. It is not individual negligence so much as a system operating beyond its monitoring capacity.
In this case, it took one year and four antibiotic courses before the pattern was identified. The patient was seen every month.
For AI systems designed to assist in clinical decision-making, this represents one of the highest-value use cases: not diagnosis, but vigilance.
