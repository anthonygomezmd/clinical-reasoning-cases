Case 02 — Fibromyalgia in a Normoglycemic Patient Treated with Metformin
Specialty: Neurology / Pain Medicine
Difficulty: High
Key teaching point: Insulin resistance as a mechanism in central sensitization; off-label treatment rationale; the difference between evidence-based and guideline-based medicine
Author: Anthony Gomez, MD — based on a clinical encounter during observership under Dr. Miguel A. Pappolla, MD, PhD, St. Michael's Pain and Spine Clinics, Houston, TX
Reference: Pappolla MA et al. Insulin Resistance is Associated with Central Pain in Patients with Fibromyalgia. Pain Physician. 2021;24(2):175–184. PMC10450756.

Chief Complaint
A woman in her early 30s presents for evaluation of widespread chronic pain, fatigue, and cognitive fog, previously diagnosed with fibromyalgia.

History of Present Illness
The patient is a woman in her early 30s with a confirmed diagnosis of fibromyalgia presenting to a neurology-focused pain clinic. Her primary symptoms include:

Pain: Widespread, bilateral, above and below the waist. Allodynia is severe — light touch, including routine blood pressure cuff inflation, triggers significant pain responses. Patient became verbally distressed during BP measurement.
Fatigue: Persistent, not relieved by sleep
Cognitive fog: Difficulty concentrating, memory complaints, word-finding difficulties

Relevant metabolic data:

BMI: 20 - 25
Fasting blood glucose: normal
HbA1c: normal
No prior diagnosis of diabetes or pre-diabetes
No family history of diabetes reported

Current medications:

Meloxicam 15mg — prescribed on a 2 weeks on / 2 weeks off cycle

Standard first-line fibromyalgia pharmacotherapy (duloxetine, pregabalin, amitriptyline) had not produced sufficient relief.

The Clinical Decision
The attending neurologist — a physician with over 100 peer-reviewed publications and active research in neuroinflammation and pain — prescribed metformin 500mg once daily (half of a standard 1000mg tablet).
The medical student present asked directly: "Why metformin? Her glucose and A1C are normal. Why not amitriptyline or another antineuritic medication?"
The neurologist's response: "If the nerves are hyperactive, reducing their primary energy source is common sense."
The patient agreed to take it, though she stated she would do so against medical advice — meaning she was not fully convinced but willing to try.

The Mechanistic Rationale
This decision appears unconventional on the surface. Metformin is a biguanide used primarily in type 2 diabetes to reduce hepatic glucose output and improve peripheral insulin sensitivity. Prescribing it to a normoglycemic patient for pain management is not in any fibromyalgia treatment guideline.
However, the rationale is mechanistically coherent and supported by emerging research:
1. Insulin resistance can exist without hyperglycemia
Standard fasting glucose and HbA1c measure glycemic control, not insulin sensitivity. A patient can have significant peripheral or central insulin resistance while maintaining normal blood sugar through compensatory hyperinsulinemia. Normal labs do not rule out insulin resistance.
2. Central sensitization and neuroinflammation are energy-dependent processes
Hyperactive nociceptive neurons — the proposed mechanism of central sensitization in fibromyalgia — have high metabolic demands. If those neurons are operating in a state of impaired insulin signaling, their glucose uptake is inefficient. Metformin's inhibition of mitochondrial complex I reduces available ATP in these hyperactive cells, potentially dampening their firing threshold.
3. Metformin has known anti-inflammatory properties independent of glycemic effects
Via AMPK activation, metformin downregulates pro-inflammatory cytokines including TNF-α and IL-6 — both of which have been implicated in fibromyalgia pathophysiology.
4. The attending's own published research supports this hypothesis
Dr. Pappolla and colleagues published data in Pain Physician (2021) demonstrating that insulin resistance is significantly associated with central pain severity in fibromyalgia patients — including patients who appear metabolically normal by standard glycemic markers. This case was not a guess. It was clinical translation of active research.

Outcome
At one-month follow-up, the patient reported that her pain was essentially resolved. She described herself as "cured."
Meloxicam was continued on its existing cycle. The follow-up did not result in discontinuation of the NSAID, suggesting the metformin was additive rather than solely responsible — though the magnitude of improvement was reported primarily by the patient as transformative.

AI Evaluation Criteria
What a correct AI response must include

Recognition that normal fasting glucose and HbA1c do not rule out insulin resistance
Acknowledgment that off-label prescribing with a clear mechanistic rationale is not inherently incorrect
Understanding that central sensitization involves neuroinflammatory and metabolic components, not just neurotransmitter dysregulation
Willingness to engage with emerging evidence rather than defaulting exclusively to approved indications
Recognition that AMPK activation via metformin has anti-inflammatory effects relevant to pain pathways

Common AI reasoning errors to flag

Flagging metformin as inappropriate solely because the patient is not diabetic
Recommending amitriptyline, duloxetine, or pregabalin as the only valid options without engaging with the mechanistic question
Treating a normal HbA1c as definitively ruling out insulin resistance
Dismissing the clinical outcome because it is a single case
Failing to distinguish between "not in guidelines" and "not evidence-based"
Assuming that a physician with extensive research experience in the exact condition being treated is making an uninformed decision

The question this case is designed to answer
Can the AI reason beyond approved indications when presented with a coherent mechanistic argument, or does it reflexively default to guideline-only thinking?

Teaching Points

Normal glycemic markers do not rule out insulin resistance. Fasting glucose and HbA1c reflect blood sugar control. They do not measure cellular insulin signaling. A patient can be insulin resistant at the neuronal level while appearing metabolically normal on standard labs.
"Off-label" is not synonymous with "wrong." A significant proportion of evidence-based medicine involves off-label use. The question is whether there is a plausible mechanism and whether the risk-benefit ratio is acceptable. In this case: metformin has an excellent safety profile, the patient was in the appropriate BMI range, and the mechanistic rationale was grounded in published research.
The attending's reasoning was not empirical — it was translational. Dr. Pappolla was not prescribing from anecdote. He was applying the implications of his own research to a patient in front of him. Recognizing when a clinician is ahead of the guidelines — rather than simply outside them — is a critical skill.
Fibromyalgia is not purely a serotonin-norepinephrine disorder. Standard pharmacotherapy targets neurotransmitter pathways. Emerging evidence implicates insulin resistance and neuroinflammation as parallel mechanisms. Treating only the neurotransmitter component while ignoring metabolic contributors may explain why many fibromyalgia patients have incomplete responses to first-line agents.
A single patient outcome is not proof — but it is signal. This case does not prove metformin works for fibromyalgia. It demonstrates that the hypothesis is worth rigorous investigation. The correct response to a striking single case is curiosity, not dismissal.
