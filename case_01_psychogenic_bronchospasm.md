Case 01 — Recurrent Airway Episodes in an 8-Year-Old
Specialty: Pediatrics / Family Medicine / Psychosomatic Medicine
Difficulty: High
Key teaching point: Psychogenic bronchospasm; the danger of anchoring bias and incomplete social history
Author: Anthony Gomez, MD — based on a real clinical encounter, IMSS Family Medicine Clinic, Tijuana, Mexico

Chief Complaint
An 8-year-old female presents to family medicine follow-up after another episode of spontaneous airway closure with cyanosis.

History of Present Illness
The patient is an 8-year-old girl brought in by both parents following a recurrent episode of what has been described as spontaneous airway obstruction with central and peripheral cyanosis. Episodes have been occurring for several months. Each episode resolves spontaneously within 30–40 minutes without any intervention. There is no loss of consciousness, no fever, no recent infection, and no identifiable environmental trigger noted by prior physicians.
Prior workup and treatment:

Evaluated initially by a family medicine physician → diagnosed with asthma → prescribed salbutamol (partial response)
Referred to ENT → diagnosed with persistent moderate asthma without inter-crisis spirometry (FEV1 not obtained) → referred to allergology
Allergology: skin prick test performed → positive for multiple allergens → started on desloratadine, placed on strict elimination diet
Despite this workup spanning multiple specialists, episodes continued

The parents present today frustrated. They have followed every recommendation. They have spent significant money on specialist care. Their child is on a strict diet and daily antihistamine. She is still having episodes.

Social History — The History Everyone Missed
Both parents work approximately 80 hours per week. The child is an only child. She has every material comfort a child could want but spends the majority of her time without her parents present.
During the clinical interview, the physician spoke directly with the child. The child began to cry, stating that she misses her parents and that despite having everything, she does not have them.

Physical Examination (Inter-Crisis)

General: well-appearing, no acute distress, appropriate for age
Respiratory: clear to auscultation bilaterally, no wheeze, no stridor at rest
Cardiovascular: regular rate and rhythm, no murmurs
Skin: no urticaria, no angioedema
Neurological: intact, appropriate affect (tearful during interview)


Diagnostic Reasoning
Broad differential at presentation

Allergic asthma
Vocal cord dysfunction (VCD) / inducible laryngeal obstruction
Foreign body aspiration (less likely given age and chronicity)
Cardiac arrhythmia with cyanosis (less likely given spontaneous resolution)
Psychogenic bronchospasm / somatization disorder
Munchausen by proxy (considered and ruled out — parents were genuinely distressed and unaware of psychological etiology)

Why the prior diagnoses were incomplete
The diagnosis of asthma was made clinically and never confirmed with spirometry during an inter-crisis period. FEV1 was not obtained. The skin prick test showed sensitization to allergens, but sensitization is not the same as clinical allergy — a positive skin test in a child with no clear allergen-triggered episodes and complete spontaneous resolution should prompt caution before attributing causality.
The strict elimination diet placed a significant burden on an already socially isolated child with no confirmed food-triggered episodes. The desloratadine addressed a finding on a test, not a symptom.
The key diagnostic question no prior physician asked
What is happening in this child's life?
A thorough social history revealed the true diagnosis: the child had learned — not consciously or deliberately — that these episodes reliably produced her parents' presence and undivided attention. The brain had established a conditioned response. This is a well-documented pediatric phenomenon: psychogenic or functional bronchospasm driven by an unmet attachment need.
The cyanosis was real. The airway closure was real. The mechanism was not allergic or structural — it was psychogenic.

Management

Parents counseled privately regarding the diagnosis of psychogenic bronchospasm secondary to attachment deprivation
No blame assigned — parents were not negligent, they were working to provide materially; they were unaware of the psychological impact
Mother transitioned to a work-from-home position
Increased parental presence and quality time implemented
Follow-up at one month: zero episodes
Elimination diet discontinued at one month — no adverse effects
Follow-up at two months: zero episodes
Desloratadine discontinued; salbutamol retained as PRN only


AI Evaluation Criteria
An AI response to this case should be evaluated against the following:
What a correct response must include

Recognition that a positive skin prick test indicates sensitization, not confirmed clinical allergy
Recognition that asthma should not be diagnosed without spirometric confirmation (FEV1/FVC) in a child of this age
Inclusion of psychogenic/functional etiology in the differential for episodic, spontaneously resolving airway symptoms
Emphasis on the importance of social history, family dynamics, and psychosocial context in pediatric cases
Understanding that treatment of a finding (positive skin test) rather than a symptom is a clinical error

Common AI reasoning errors to flag

Anchoring on the prior asthma diagnosis without questioning its validity
Treating a positive skin prick test as confirmatory of allergic disease
Failing to include psychogenic bronchospasm in the differential
Recommending escalation of allergy workup without first obtaining spirometry
Missing the social history entirely or treating it as irrelevant to the diagnosis
Recommending continuation of strict dietary elimination without confirmed food-triggered episodes

The question this case is designed to answer
Can the AI recognize when a diagnosis has been anchored incorrectly by prior providers, and can it identify psychosocial context as diagnostically relevant?

Teaching Points

Anchoring bias is dangerous in referral chains. Each specialist inherited the prior diagnosis and built on it rather than questioning it. The family medicine physician was the first to return to first principles.
A positive test is not a diagnosis. Skin prick test positivity indicates IgE sensitization. It does not confirm that allergen exposure is causing the patient's symptoms.
Spirometry is required to diagnose asthma. Clinical diagnosis alone — especially without inter-crisis FEV1 — is insufficient and leads to overdiagnosis.
In pediatric patients with unexplained recurrent symptoms, the social history is part of the physical exam. Family dynamics, parental availability, and the child's emotional environment are diagnostic data.
Psychogenic symptoms are real symptoms. The cyanosis and airway closure were physiologically real. Dismissing them as "not real" would have been as harmful as missing the diagnosis.
The least invasive intervention was the most effective. No medication change. No further specialist referral. A mother working from home resolved what three specialists and multiple medications could not.
